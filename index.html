Core Clinical Patterns — Single Organ System

Each pattern represents a constellation of findings that share the same root cause. When the movement screen shows the first finding, test the listed muscles. When those muscles are inhibited, treat the organ factor. The pattern confirms itself when treating the organ restores the muscle immediately on retest.

01
Kidney / Adrenal Pattern
The most common pattern in clinical practice — stress, fatigue, chronic pain
Screen Finds

Test These

Organ / NL
Kidney / Adrenal Glands
NL Anterior: 1" above umbilicus, 1" lateral (bilateral). NL Posterior: T11–T12 paraspinal. Classic presentation: chronic stress patient, poor sleep, low energy.
Spinal
T12–L2 Check T12-L1 for restriction — the most common fixation in kidney pattern patients. Also check OA joint (upper trap / atlas connection).
Meridian
Kidney (KI) ▶ START: KI-27 KI-27 inferior to medial clavicle. Also CV-6 (2 cun below navel) for adrenal tonification. KI-3 (medial ankle) for direct kidney Qi access. Test psoas → stimulate KI-27 → retest psoas. Change is immediate if kidney is the factor.
Pattern Note
When psoas AND gastroc are both inhibited, adrenal burden is the primary suspect. This patient often presents with bilateral low back pain, heel pain, neck tension, and reports "always tired." Treat the kidney/adrenal first — everything else may follow.

Large Intestine / Colon
NL Anterior: IT band (lateral thigh, top third). NL Posterior: L3–L4 paraspinal. Ask about bowel regularity. Constipation, IBS, bloating frequently present in this pattern. Bilateral glute inhibition = suspect colon first.
Spinal
L5–S1 SI joint fixation is the most common structural finding. L5-S1 disc involvement also common. Adjust SI joint + L5-S1 first, then retest glute max.
Meridian

Pattern Note
The TFL is frequently overactive in this pattern — it is compensating for the inhibited glute max. Do not treat the TFL directly. Restore the glute and the TFL releases on its own. Treating TFL without addressing the LI connection is why patients keep coming back every week.

Patterns 02 and 03 often coexist — the patient has both glute max and glute med inhibited. In that case, treat LI-4 first (glute max), then SI-3 (glute med). The sequence matters. The bigger motor muscle (glute max) is the foundation of pelvic stability and should restore first.

Spleen / Pancreas

This is frequently the missing piece in chronic shoulder patients who have "done everything." They've been given serratus and lower trap exercises for months with minimal response. Address T6-T8 and SP-3, and the lower trap will fire in the same visit.

Compound Clinical Presentations

Real patients rarely present with one clean pattern. These compound presentations are the most common multi-system constellations seen in clinical practice. Each one involves multiple organ systems simultaneously — knowing the compound pattern tells you which meridian to treat first.

COMPOUND A Chronic Low Back Pain — The Triple Inhibition
What Screen Shows
  • Lumbar flexion substitution on hip hinge
  • Trendelenburg on single leg squat
  • Lateral shift toward one side on squat
  • Glute max does not fire first on prone hip extension test
  • SI area pain and tenderness
Muscles Involved
  • Psoas inhibited — Kidney (Pattern 01)
  • Glute max inhibited — Large Intestine (Pattern 02)
  • Glute med inhibited — Small Intestine (Pattern 03)
  • Erector spinae overactive (compensating for all three)
  • TFL overactive (compensating for glute max)
Treatment Sequence
  • 1st: KI-27 → restore psoas → retest
  • 2nd: LI-4 → restore glute max → retest
  • 3rd: SI-3 → restore glute med → retest
  • Structural: SI joint, L5-S1, L4-L5
  • Load: Hip hinge pattern immediately after
Clinical Insight: The sequence matters here. Psoas (kidney) is the foundational spinal stabilizer — restore it first. Then glute max (LI) as the primary hip extensor. Then glute med (SI) for frontal plane stability. Treating them out of sequence produces partial results. All three meridians — KI, LI, SI — should respond in the same visit in a genuine triple inhibition pattern.
COMPOUND B Chronic Shoulder Impingement — The Four-System Shoulder
What Screen Shows
  • Scapular winging on wall push
  • Arms drop forward on overhead squat
  • Wall angel: hands leave early
  • Scapula shrugs instead of rotating
  • Limited shoulder IR (subscapularis)
Muscles Involved
  • Serratus ant. inhibited — Lung (Pattern 06)
  • Lower trap inhibited — Spleen (Pattern 05)
  • Subscapularis inhibited — Heart (Pattern 08)
  • Infraspinatus inhibited — TW (Pattern 10)
  • Upper trap overactive (compensating for all)
Treatment Sequence
  • 1st: LU-1 → serratus → scapular base
  • 2nd: SP-3 + T6-T8 adjust → lower trap
  • 3rd: HT-7 → subscapularis
  • 4th: TW-5 → infraspinatus
  • Load: Serratus wall push → row → press
Clinical Insight: Four different organ systems driving the same shoulder presentation. This is why isolated rotator cuff rehab fails — it treats the muscles as if they are purely biomechanical problems, when three different meridians are suppressing the stabilizers. The thoracic adjustment (T6-T8) is often the single highest-yield structural intervention because it simultaneously releases the thoracic fixation that blocks lower trap AND unlocks the mid-thoracic spleen NL points.

The patient typically reports chronic fatigue, poor stress tolerance, difficulty recovering from exercise, and bilateral low back + heel pain. This is your GLP-1 patient, your post-COVID patient, your burned-out executive. The kidney/adrenal system is exhausted and everything connected to it has gone offline.

Clinical Decision Flow — Every Visit
1
Movement Screen (60 sec)
Gross snapshot. Overhead squat, SLS step-down, hip hinge, wall angel. Identify the primary movement fault. Note which direction and which side.
2
Identify Muscle Targets
Use the screen-to-muscle map. The fault tells you which 2-3 muscles to test first. Don't test everything — start with the most likely suspects for that fault pattern.
3
Muscle Test in Shortened Position
Approximate origin to insertion. Challenge with break test. Binary result: facilitated or inhibited. If inhibited, proceed to the 5 factors.
4
Run 5 Factors
O&I stimulation → Organ/NL → Spinal level → Meridian point. Retest after each factor. The factor that restores the muscle is the root cause. Stop there — don't treat factors that aren't involved.
5
Structural Treatment
Manipulate the fixated segment identified in factor 4. Immediately retest the inhibited muscle. Movement should improve in the neurological window that follows.
6
Functional Load
Immediately load the restored muscle in the functional position that mirrors its real-world demand. This cements the nerve-muscle communication into the movement pattern.
7
Rescreen
Repeat the movement test that was faulty in step 1. The movement should change measurably. Show the patient the change. This is your proof of mechanism and their buy-in.
The Most Important Rule in This System
When a muscle restores to a specific factor, that factor tells you the root cause. Do not treat all 5 factors. If KI-27 restores the psoas, treat the kidney. If LI-4 restores the glute max, treat the colon. The factor that works tells you the whole story. Treating unnecessary factors dilutes your clinical signal and wastes your patient's time.
Pattern Priority — Where to Start When Overwhelmed

When a patient has multiple findings and you're not sure where to start, use this priority order. Certain patterns, when treated first, cascade positively into other patterns.

If it is depleted, every other pattern will be partially suppressed and respond incompletely. Psoas, gastroc, soleus, upper trap all potentially restore. Patient energy improves. Often dramatic.
